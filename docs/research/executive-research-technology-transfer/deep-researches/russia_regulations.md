  "content": "# Russia 152‑FZ Data‑Localization & CBR AI Regulations (2024 Update)\n\n## 1.  Federal Law No. 152‑FZ – Data‑Localization Requirements  \n\n### 1.1 Core obligations for banks and other financial institutions  \n* **Territorial scope** – The law applies to any legal entity that collects personal data of Russian citizens in Russia, including foreign banks that have a legal presence or deliberately target Russian customers .  \n* **Operator vs. processor** – Both the “operator” (the entity that decides the purposes of processing) and any third‑party “processor” acting on its behalf must ensure that the entire life‑cycle of the data (recording, systematisation, storage, updating, retrieval) is carried out on databases located inside the Russian Federation .  \n* **Exceptions** – Processing abroad is permitted only in the limited cases listed in clauses 2, 3, 4 and 8 of part 1 of Article 6 (e.g., international agreements, legal proceedings, state‑body powers, journalistic activities) .\n\n### 1.2 Cross‑border personal‑data transfers  \n* **Notification to Roskomnadzor** – Before any transfer, the controller must submit a written notification describing the recipient, security measures, and purpose. For countries deemed “adequate,” the transfer may start immediately; otherwise, explicit approval (or the lapse of a statutory deadline) is required [1].  \n* **Post‑July 2025 regime** – The amendment entering force on 1 July 2025 turns the previous “positive obligation” into a **prohibition** on storing Russian citizens’ data abroad, except for the same four statutory exceptions . Practically, this means that even a copy of data kept on foreign servers is barred unless an exception applies [2].\n\n### 1.3 Penalties and enforcement  \n* **Administrative fines** – First‑time violations attract up to RUB 6 million; repeated offences up to RUB 18 million .  \n* **Increased fines (effective 30 May 2025)** – Minimum fines rise to RUB 150 000–300 000 for initial breaches and up to RUB 500 000 for repeated breaches [1].  \n* **Supervisory body** – Roskomnadzor conducts remote monitoring of web‑based data‑collection points and may issue enforcement notices; non‑compliance can also trigger civil liability under the draft law on dismissing information‑security officers for data breaches that is under parliamentary discussion [3].\n\n## 2.  Central Bank of Russia (CBR) – AI Regulations for the Financial Sector  \n\n### 2.1 Regulatory framework (2024‑2025)  \n* **Code of Ethics for AI in Finance** – A voluntary, risk‑oriented code that sets principles of transparency, reliability and data protection [4].  \n* **Consultation paper (Dec 2025 deadline)** – Calls for creation of “trusted‑intermediary” platforms for voluntary data exchange and joint AI‑model development, and invites comments on privacy‑enhancing technologies [4].  \n* **Federal Law No. 123‑FZ (AI Liability, July 2024)** – Introduces mandatory civil‑liability insurance for AI developers operating in experimental legal regimes, with stricter requirements for high‑risk financial‑service applications [5].  \n* **Experimental Legal Regimes (ELR)** – A sandbox‑style framework allowing AI pilots under temporary regulatory exemptions, overseen by a coordination council that includes the CBR [6].\n\n### 2.2 Compliance obligations for banks  \n| Obligation | Practical requirement |\n|------------|-----------------------|\n| **AI‑risk taxonomy** – AI risk must be classified (high‑risk, restricted‑risk) and managed as a standalone risk category [7]. |\n| **Model validation & documentation** – Banks must maintain detailed model cards, data‑lineage records, and impact‑assessment reports before deployment. |\n| **Insurance** – For high‑risk AI applications, banks must obtain civil‑liability insurance in line with Law 123‑FZ. |\n| **Reporting & supervision** – Periodic reports on AI‑risk metrics are submitted to the CBR; the regulator conducts audits and may issue supervisory directives. |\n| **Data localisation** – Training data that contain personal data of Russian citizens must reside in Russian‑located databases; any foreign AI service that accesses such data must do so via “access‑only” mechanisms (e.g., remote query) rather than data export [8]. |\n\n### 2.3 Cross‑border AI model deployment  \n* **Access‑only transfers** – After localisation, foreign AI providers may be granted remote access to Russian‑hosted data (e.g., via API) if Roskomnadzor does not object [2].  \n* **Full export prohibited** – Storing a copy of the training data abroad is barred under the July 2025 amendment to 152‑FZ, unless an exception applies.  \n* **Trusted‑intermediary platforms** – The CBR encourages market participants to create shared Russian‑based AI‑model repositories that can be accessed by multiple banks while keeping the underlying data local [4].\n\n### 2.4 Enforcement & penalties  \n* **Supervisory monitoring** – The CBR monitors AI‑risk management practices, publishes best‑practice collections, and may require remediation actions.  \n* **Sanctions** – Non‑compliance with the AI Code of Ethics or insurance requirements can lead to supervisory fines, restrictions on licence renewals, or revocation of sandbox participation [5].  \n* **Study of systemic risk** – The CBR’s 2026‑2028 research agenda includes assessing AI’s impact on financial stability, indicating that future supervisory actions may be tied to macro‑prudential findings [9].\n\n## 3.  Interaction of Data‑Localization and AI Rules  \n\n| Issue | Combined regulatory impact |\n|-------|-----------------------------|\n| **Training data** | Personal data used to train AI models must be stored and processed on Russian servers (152‑FZ) before any model‑training activity (CBR AI risk framework). |\n| **Model export** | Exporting a trained model that embeds personal data is effectively a cross‑border transfer of personal data and is prohibited unless the model is “de‑identified” and an exception applies. |\n| **Cloud services** | Foreign cloud providers can be used only if they host data in Russian data‑centres or provide “access‑only” interfaces; otherwise banks must migrate workloads to domestic cloud operators. |\n| **Sandbox participation** | AI pilots in ELRs must submit a data‑localisation compliance plan and obtain insurance under Law 123‑FZ before the sandbox is approved. |\n| **Risk‑management systems** | AI‑risk modules must integrate checks for localisation compliance (e.g., flagging any outbound data flow) to satisfy both 152‑FZ and CBR supervisory expectations. |\n\n## 4.  Practical Implications for Financial Institutions  \n\n1. **Technology architecture** – Banks need Russian‑based data lakes and compute clusters; hybrid architectures must enforce “read‑only” remote access for foreign AI services.  \n2. **Vendor contracts** – Service‑level agreements must include clauses guaranteeing data‑localisation, Roskomnadzor notification procedures, and mandatory AI‑liability insurance.  \n3. **Compliance programmes** – Separate but coordinated teams for personal‑data protection (handling Roskomnadzor notifications, breach reporting) and AI‑risk (model validation, insurance) are required.  \n4. **Capital & operational budgeting** – Anticipate additional costs for domestic data‑centre capacity, insurance premiums for high‑risk AI, and potential fines (up to RUB 18 million for localisation breaches).  \n5. **Strategic choices** – Investing in Russian AI‑platforms or joining trusted‑intermediary initiatives can reduce reliance on foreign providers and lower regulatory risk. Participation in ELRs offers a controlled path to innovate while obtaining temporary regulatory exemptions.  \n\n## 5.  Summary  \n\n- **Data‑localisation** under 152‑FZ remains a strict requirement, with a 2025 amendment turning the rule into an outright prohibition on storing Russian citizens’ personal data abroad, subject to limited statutory exceptions and a notification‑approval process via Roskomnadzor.  \n- **AI regulations** issued by the CBR in 2024‑2025 (Code of Ethics, Law 123‑FZ, ELR sandbox framework) impose risk‑management, insurance, and reporting duties on banks that deploy AI in credit, payments, or risk analytics.  \n- **Cross‑border activities** are tightly constrained: foreign AI services may only access Russian‑localised data remotely; any export of personal data or models containing such data is generally prohibited.  \n- **Enforcement** combines administrative fines (up to RUB 18 million for localisation breaches, escalating penalties from 2025) and CBR supervisory actions (fines, licence restrictions, mandatory remediation).  \n- **Operational impact** forces financial institutions to redesign data infrastructures, embed localisation checks into AI‑risk frameworks, secure insurance for high‑risk AI, and align technology roadmaps with domestic‑only or “access‑only” solutions.  \n\nCompliance with both regimes is essential for continued operation in the Russian financial market and for mitigating regulatory, financial, and reputational risks associated with data‑localisation and AI deployment.\n\n---\n\n### Sources\n- [1] https://www.dlapiperdataprotection.com/index.html?t=law&c=RU\n- [2] https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=28f1e711-dc2d-4110-a0f5-f4d3d9d47b76\n- [3] https://www.alrud.ru/storage/article_data/4341/3164/ALRUD_Data_Protection_What_Do_Operators_Need_To_Know_in_2024.pdf\n- [4] https://www.cbr.ru/eng/press/event/?id=28109\n- [5] https://regulations.ai/regulations/russia-summary\n- [6] https://regulations.ai/regulations/RAI-RU-NA-FELRSXX-2020\n- [7] https://b1.ru/local/assets/surveys/b1-cro-survey-trends-in-banking-risk-management-en.pdf\n- [8] https://www.cbr.ru/eng/fintech/\n- [9] https://tass.com/economy/2087479\n",
