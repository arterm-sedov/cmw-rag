  "content": "# GPU‑Utilisation Thresholds for On‑Premises vs Cloud TCO Break‑Even  \n\n## 1. Why the utilisation rate matters  \nThe total cost of ownership (TCO) of a GPU‑centric AI workload is the sum of **capital expenses (CapEx)** – hardware purchase, installation and financing – and **operating expenses (OpEx)** – power, cooling, rack space, staff, maintenance, software licences and any cloud‑specific fees.  \nBecause the **marginal cost of running a GPU is almost zero once the hardware is paid for**, the proportion of time the GPU is actually doing useful work (its utilisation) determines whether the fixed on‑premises spend is justified compared with the purely pay‑as‑you‑go cloud model.  \n\n---\n\n## 2. Methodology & Core Formulae  \n\n| Symbol | Meaning |\n|--------|---------|\n| **C_cap** | Total CapEx for the GPU system (hardware + infrastructure) |\n| **L** | Economic life of the hardware (years) – typically 3‑5 y for AI GPUs |\n| **A_cap** | Annualised capital cost = C_cap ÷ L |\n| **C_op** | Annual OpEx (power, cooling, facility, staff, support, software) |\n| **C_on‑prem,hr** | On‑premises cost per GPU‑hour = (A_cap + C_op) ÷ (8760 × N_gpu) |\n| **C_cloud,hr** | Cloud cost per GPU‑hour (on‑demand, reserved or spot) |\n| **U\\*** | Utilisation break‑even = C_on‑prem,hr ÷ C_cloud,hr |\n| **T_break‑even** | Hours of GPU use needed for total cost parity = C_cap ÷ (C_cloud,hr − C_on‑prem,hr) ÷ N_gpu |\n\nAll cost items are taken from publicly disclosed calculators, vendor pricing sheets and recent case studies (see citations). The calculations are performed for a **typical 8‑GPU H100 server** – a common baseline for enterprise AI clusters – and then varied to show sensitivity.  \n\n---\n\n## 3. Baseline Cost Components  \n\n### 3.1 On‑Premises (8 × NVIDIA H100 SXM, 6.1 kW rack)  \n\n| Item | Cost (5‑year horizon) | Source |\n|------|-----------------------|--------|\n| GPU hardware (8 × H100) | $216 000 (≈ $27 000 per GPU) | [1] |\n| Server, network, power‑&‑cooling infra. | $76 710 (servers + network + cooling) | [1] |\n| **Total CapEx** | **$292 710** | [1] |\n| Annual OpEx – electricity (6.1 kW × 8760 h × $0.12/kWh × PUE 1.3) | $7 695 | [1] |\n| Facility/colocation | $13 176 | [1] |\n| Personnel (0.5 FTE) | $50 000 | [1] |\n| Maintenance & support (≈ 30 % of hardware) | $32 600 | [1] |\n| **Annual OpEx total** | **$103 471** | [1] |\n| **Annualised capital** (5 y) | $58 542 | ( $292 710 ÷ 5 ) |\n| **On‑prem cost per GPU‑hour** | **≈ $2.40** ( $58 542 + $103 471 ) ÷ (8760 h × 8) | [1] |\n\n### 3.2 Cloud (per‑GPU pricing, March 2026)  \n\n| Provider / Instance | On‑Demand $/hr | Reserved (1‑yr all‑upfront) discount 20‑40 % | Spot discount 60‑90 % | Source |\n|---------------------|----------------|--------------------------------------------|----------------------|--------|\n| AWS p5.48xlarge (8 × H100) | $6.88 / GPU hr | $4.80 / GPU hr (≈30 % off) | $0.70 / GPU hr (≈90 % off) |  |\n| GCP a3‑ultragpu (8 × H100) | $5.78 / GPU hr | $3.50 / GPU hr (≈40 % off) | $0.58 / GPU hr | [2] |\n| Azure NC24ads (8 × H100) | $3.67 / GPU hr | $2.20 / GPU hr (≈40 % off) | $0.55 / GPU hr |  |\n\n*Only the on‑demand and 1‑yr reserved rates are used for the break‑even analysis; spot pricing is relevant for bursty, non‑critical workloads (see Section 6).*\n\n---\n\n## 4. Break‑Even Utilisation Calculations  \n\n### 4.1 On‑Demand Cloud  \n\n\\[\nU^{*}_{\\text{on‑demand}} = \\frac{C_{\\text{on‑prem,hr}}}{C_{\\text{cloud,hr}}}\n= \\frac{2.40}{6.88} \\approx 0.35 \\;(35\\%)\n\\]\n\nSo **≈ 35 % average utilisation** makes on‑premises cheaper than paying on‑demand cloud rates.  \n\n### 4.2 1‑Year Reserved Cloud  \n\n\\[\nU^{*}_{\\text{reserved}} = \\frac{2.40}{4.80} \\approx 0.50 \\;(50\\%)\n\\]\n\nWith a typical 30 % reserved discount, the break‑even rises to **≈ 50 % utilisation**.  \n\n### 4.3 Spot (bursty)  \n\nIf spot pricing is used (average $0.70 / GPU hr), the cloud is cheaper even at **< 10 % utilisation**, confirming that spot is the right choice for highly intermittent workloads.  \n\n### 4.4 Alternative Example – Lenovo 8 × H200 (2026)  \n\nLenovo’s 8‑GPU H200 system has a 5‑year TCO of $662 900 (CapEx + OpEx) and a per‑hour cost of $8.79 (including power, cooling and colocation) [3]. Using Google Cloud on‑demand $84.81 / hour for the equivalent 8‑GPU instance [3], the break‑even utilisation is:\n\n\\[\nU^{*} = \\frac{8.79}{84.81} \\approx 0.10 \\;(10\\%)\n\\]\n\nBecause the H200 is far more powerful per‑watt, the utilisation threshold collapses to **≈ 10 %** for that specific hardware generation. This illustrates how newer GPU architectures shift the break‑even dramatically lower.  \n\n---\n\n## 5. Sensitivity Analysis  \n\n| Variable | Base Assumption | Effect on U\\* (higher = more utilisation needed) |\n|----------|----------------|---------------------------------------------------|\n| **Electricity price** | $0.12 /kWh (US average 2025) | Raising to $0.19 /kWh (2026 average) adds ≈ $0.30 / GPU‑hr → U\\* rises from 35 % to ~42 % (on‑demand) |\n| **PUE** | 1.3 (efficient colocation) | Worse PUE = 1.5 adds ≈ 15 % to power cost → U\\* ↑ ≈ 5 % |\n| **Hardware refresh cycle** | 5 y (straight‑line depreciation) | Shortening to 3 y increases annualised capital → U\\* ↑ ≈ 7 % |\n| **Reserved‑instance discount** | 30 % off on‑demand | Deeper 40 % discount → U\\* ↓ ≈ 5 % (≈ 45 % → 45 %→ 45 %? on‑premises would sit idle > 80 % of the time |\n| **Real‑time inference at high QPS** (e.g., recommendation engine) | 60‑90 % (GPU‑dense) | On‑premises recommended for latency‑critical, high‑throughput services; otherwise hybrid (baseline on‑prem, spikes in cloud) |\n| **Batch inference with low utilisation** | 10‑30 % | Cloud spot or serverless inference APIs are far cheaper |\n\nMulti‑tenant scheduling on‑premises (e.g., sharing GPUs across teams, using container‑orchestrated workloads) can lift effective utilisation from the 30 % baseline to the 50‑70 % range, moving the economics toward on‑premises (see SoftwareSeni analysis of 35 %‑70 % MFU) [4].  \n\n---\n\n## 7. Practical Decision Framework  \n\n1. **Estimate average GPU utilisation** (hours per day ÷ 24).  \n2. **Select the cloud pricing tier** you would realistically use (on‑demand, 1‑yr reserved, spot).  \n3. Compute **C_on‑prem,hr** using your own CapEx (hardware list, financing rate, expected life) plus OpEx (power, PUE, staff, support).  \n4. Compare **U\\*** from Section 4 with your estimated utilisation:  \n\n| Utilisation % | Decision |\n|---------------|----------|\n| **< 30 %** (bursty) | Cloud spot / on‑demand is cheaper; avoid large CapEx. |\n| **30 % – 45 %** | On‑premises beats on‑demand but not reserved; consider a hybrid model (baseline on‑prem, reserve cloud for peak). |\n| **45 % – 60 %** | On‑premises beats both on‑demand and typical 1‑yr reserved contracts; purchase if you have 3‑5 y horizon. |\n| **> 60 %** (steady) | Strong case for on‑premises; also evaluate financing/lease options to preserve cash (operating lease ≈ $0.61 / GPU‑hr at 80 % utilisation, see Introl blog) [5]. |\n\n### Hybrid Recommendations  \n\n| Scenario | Suggested Mix |\n|----------|---------------|\n| **Baseline inference ≈ 30 %**, occasional training spikes ≈ 70 %** | Keep a modest on‑prem cluster (e.g., 4 × H100) for inference; burst training on reserved or spot cloud. |\n| **Development environment with many short experiments** | Cloud‑first (spot) for experimentation; purchase on‑prem only after usage stabilises ≥ 50 %. |\n| **Regulated data (cannot leave premises)** | On‑premises for all workloads; factor in higher staff cost and possible higher electricity rates (up to $0.19/kWh) – still break‑even at ~40 % utilisation (see Bloomberg electricity analysis) [6]. |\n\n---\n\n## 8. Key Take‑aways  \n\n* **On‑premises becomes cheaper than cloud on‑demand at roughly **35 %** average GPU utilisation and cheaper than a 1‑year reserved contract at roughly **50 %** utilisation for a typical 8‑GPU H100 server.**  \n* Newer GPU generations (e.g., H200, Blackwell B200) with higher performance‑per‑watt can push the break‑even utilisation down to **10 %–20 %**, because the per‑hour operational cost falls dramatically (Lenovo case) [3].  \n* **Electricity price, PUE, staffing level, and hardware refresh cadence are the dominant sensitivity factors**; a 50 % rise in electricity (from $0.12 to $0.19 /kWh) lifts the break‑even from 35 % to ≈ 42 % (on‑demand).  \n* **Spot instances make cloud the cheapest option for workloads with utilisation below ~10 %**, but they are unsuitable for latency‑critical or long‑running training jobs.  \n* **Hybrid models are often optimal**: a modest on‑prem baseline for steady inference, complemented by reserved or spot cloud capacity for training spikes or seasonal inference peaks.  \n\nUse the formulas above with your own hardware prices, electricity rates and staffing assumptions to compute a customised break‑even utilisation for your environment.
